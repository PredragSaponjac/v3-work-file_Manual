Why this company is the cleanest expression:
Jet fuel is generally airlines' biggest cost after labor, accounting for 20% of expenses or more. FedEx operates the world's largest cargo airline fleet (FedEx Express), and fuel is a massive cost input. Challenges include global trade policy changes, reduced trans-Pacific capacity by 25%, and the grounding of 25 out of 34 MD-11 aircraft in the fleet. FedEx was already dealing with fleet groundings and capacity constraints entering this quarter. Unlike airlines that can pass through fuel surcharges quickly to new bookings, FedEx's Express segment has contracted/committed rates with shippers that cannot be retroactively repriced mid-quarter. The Q3 fiscal quarter ending Feb 28 captured the very beginning of the conflict, and forward guidance for Q4 (March–May) will need to address the full brunt of the fuel shock. This is a cleaner expression than passenger airlines because (a) it reports earnings imminently, (b) it has a massive air fleet with direct jet fuel exposure, and (c) the market narrative around FDX has been about transformation/margin expansion, not fuel risk.

What already moved:
Brent futures rose to close at $103.14 per barrel. U.S. crude oil settled at $98.71 per barrel. Oil prices have notched another week of gains, with Brent futures up about 10%. It follows the 27.9% rise seen last week, which marked the biggest weekly gain in oil prices since the Covid-19 pandemic in 2020. Jet fuel prices have spiked as high as 90% since January 2026. The Argus U.S. Jet Fuel Index pegged the average price at around $3.93 per gallon in early March, up from $2.07 in early January 2026. While crude oil prices have climbed by roughly one-third since the conflict intensified, jet fuel prices have risen at a much faster pace, in some cases doubling over the same period. Before the conflict, jet fuel in Asia typically traded about $21 per barrel higher than crude oil. However, the refining margin surged to as much as $144 per barrel on March 4, before easing to around $65 per barrel. Even at that lower level, the margin remains unusually high compared with historical norms.

However, this decline largely tracks the broader market selloff. What has not repriced enough:
The analyst community and stock price are still anchored to the margin-expansion/transformation narrative. Jet fuel has nearly doubled while FDX is only ~10% off highs. Jefferies analyst Sheila Kahyaoglu said she expects "the most acute financial impact to airlines from surging oil prices to be in the next 30-90 days as airlines have been booking yields for close-in flights assuming a much lower fuel price and carriers cannot retroactively raise fares." This same logic applies to FedEx's contracted Express rates. The forward guidance for fiscal Q4 (March–May 2026) will need to address $3.80+/gallon jet fuel vs. the ~$2.00–$2.50 that was baked into FY2026 guidance. Additionally, the grounding of 25 out of 34 MD-11 aircraft means FedEx is already capacity-constrained and less able to optimize fuel efficiency through fleet management.

Expected tactical payoff range (% move): 8–15% downside on earnings miss + guidance cut, based on historical FDX earnings reactions to negative surprises and the magnitude of the fuel cost increase relative to operating income.

Why this company is the cleanest expression: Century Aluminum produces primary aluminum and value-added aluminum products in the United States and Iceland. Its annual production capacity is approximately 1,016,000 tons per year. It is the purest listed U.S. smelter, selling into the domestic market at LME + Midwest Premium. Commerzbank noted that "the rise in prices is likely to have provided an incentive to expand production, especially in the US, where high prices are paired with record-high physical premiums." In 2025, the Middle East accounted for roughly 21% of unwrought aluminum imports into the U.S. — supply now partially cut off, directly reducing import competition for CENX's domestic volumes. Alcoa (AA) is more diversified across bauxite/alumina and less leveraged to the Midwest Premium on a per-share basis.
What already moved: At the outbreak of the Iran conflict on Feb. 28, 3-month LME aluminum futures initially jumped by as much as 10% by March 12 before paring some gains to land around 8% higher. Prices on the LME surged to a four-year high of $3,418 per tonne, and Citigroup warned prices could surge to $4,000/ton if supply disruptions persist. Major smelters declared force majeure, including facilities linked to Aluminium Bahrain and Norsk Hydro after gas supplies to the Qatalum smelter were suspended. The U.S. Midwest Premium surpassed $1 per pound for the first time in late January — and has likely risen further since the conflict.
What has already repriced in this stock (and approximate magnitude): CENX rose from approximately $53–54 pre-conflict to a new 52-week high of ~$58 by March 11 (+7–9%), then pulled back to ~$56.49 by March 17 — roughly +4–5% net from pre-conflict levels. Wells Fargo raised its price target from $61 to $69 on March 12.

Additionally, the record Midwest Premium — which is additive to LME for U.S. smelters — and the reduction in ME import competition (~21% of U.S. unwrought imports) represent incremental company-specific tailwinds beyond the LME move.
